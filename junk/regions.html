Major coffee producing regions

Americas

Hawaii

Notable as the only place in The United States that grows coffee, production in Hawaii is centered on the Kona mountain. Kona coffee fetches high prices, due to limited growing area and high demand. Growers have been expanding their plantings in Kona for many years, leading to reduced quality as they plant in less ideal areas.

Mexico

Mexico is a major coffee producing nation. Some Mexican coffee is extremely good, much of the best coming from Chiapas. The best Mexican coffee has a rich, nutty aroma and taste, often with a delicate but pronounced acidity. Despite a fair amount of production and a fairly reliable quality, Mexican coffee is not among the most common coffees carried as varietals. It is often used by roasters in blends.

Guatemala, Costa Rica, Nicuragua

These are generally high quality, well balanced coffees. They can be, in my opininon, among the best coffees, with a rich aroma, a well rounded complex body, and a distinctive acidity. Central American coffees are generally very similar to their much more famous Colombian and Jamaican relatives.

Colombia

Famous for its well balanced, mild coffees, Colombia is a major coffee producing region. Generally of lower quality than other high quality coffees at a given price point, Colombia is nevertheless popular and decent quality.

Brazil

The best Brazilian coffees are outstanding: nutty, rich, spicy, and delicate with an aroma that is hard to match in any coffee. Sadly, Brazil is the largest producer of low quality coffees in the world. Most Brazil coffee has an unfortunate sharp bitter note that comes through any blend it goes into. Low quality Brazil is often used in blends by coffee roasters trying to cut corners.

Africa
Ethopia

Ethiopia really should be divided into three groups, as there are three distinctive coffee growing regions in Ethiopia: Harrar, Yirgacheffe, and Sidamo.

Kenya

Fruity and sharp, an intense acidity and a weak body are the hallmarks of Kenyan coffee. Very popular, often going into espresso blends, the Kenyan crop often sells out as soon as it's harvested. Many smaller producers substitute coffee from Burundi, which has a very similar flavor profile, but is often less expensive.

Asia
Sumatra

Complex, earthy, rich aroma, full body and weak acidity are the marks of Sumatra coffee. The best Sumatra is of the Mandheling variety, which has a unique wet process for depulping the beans. Because it has such a strong body, many producers want to roast Sumatra to an extremely dark roast. The body will stand up to this, but its already weak acidity will be completely cooked away, leaving a cup with a very strong bittersweet body and virtually no acidity at all. I do not recommend taking Sumatra to a dark roast. At a light roast, Sumatra's naturally strong body will leave a cup that tastes like a darker roast than it is, while preserving its delicate acidity.

Papua New Guinea

Earthy, with a heavy body and a rich aroma, PNG is generally a workhorse coffee. It tends to be very stable from season to season, making it a common addition to blends. It doesn't have the complex body that Sumatra does, but also has a stronger acidity.

Java

Very similar to PNG, but generally a little less expensive. This was once such a famed varietal that its name is a synonym for coffee to this day. Sadly, climate change and overproduction have left Java coffee a pale shadow of what it once was.

India

There is a small amount of coffee that comes out of India, much of it fairly standard Asian fare, not of particularly high quality. There is one variety in particular, Monsoon Malabar, which is processed uniquely. After drying, it is bagged and stacked in drafty warehouses during the Indian Monsoon season. This is a highly unconventional way to store coffee, as climate control and dry, cool conditions are key to keeping green coffee in good condition. In this case, however, it lends the coffee a unique note of sandalwood, and leads to a very interesting cup of coffee.
