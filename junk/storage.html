Care and storage of coffee

Many people store their coffee poorly or incorrectly. Green coffee can store for months with no problems, but once roasted, coffee beans have a very limited lifespan. If left in whole beans, coffee will last a couple of weeks after it's roasted. If ground, it will last less long than that, based on the greater surface area exposed to the air. Ground coarsely, as for a French press, it will age about 100 times faster than in whole bean form (as it has aound 100 times the surface area now) -- so it will start to become noticeably stale in several hours. Ground very finely, as for espresso, it will age about a thousand times faster, (again, now having around a thousand times the surface area), so it should be brewed within a minute or so.

Coffee needs to "outgas%quot; as it ages. Starting the minute it comes out of the roaster, it starts to get stale. As it stales, it releases carbon dioxide gas. It only stops once it is fully stale. That is why the coffee you buy at the grocery store has vents in the bags. The contents would inflate and eventually rupture the bags without a pressure release. Any coffee that you purchase in a can is already fully stale; it must be made completely stale before it can be packaged into a can.

You can delay this action by freezing your coffee. I don't really recommend freezing, as it damages the coffee beans, but if you need to keep your coffee for more than a couple of weeks, freezing is better than leaving it out. On the other hand, moisture is the worst thing for your beans, and the condensation from the cold air in the refrigerator is far more damaging to your coffee beans than being left to outgas, so never put your coffee in the refrigerator.

Pro tip: The outgassing of extremely fresh coffee beans will create a severe turbulence when they are exposed to water. The more finely ground the coffee is, the more significant the sudden burst of outgassing will be. This makes extremely fresh coffee make very poor espresso, as it will behave unpredictably in the portafilter. To make espresso, beans need to age for three to five days before they can be used.
