Some methods for brewing coffee

This is not meant to be exhaustive, but is meant to be representative.

How much coffee to use

Most people don't put enough ground coffee beans into their coffee. It's an easy mistake to make. Not using enough grounds will make your coffee taste very bitter. Many people associate this with coffee being too strong. It seems counter intuitive, but the opposite is true, and here's why: in making coffee, the goal is not to extract all the soluble compounds from the grounds, only some of them. Too weak a brew allows the water to dissolve many undesirable compounds into your extract, which carry an excessively bitter taste.

This chart can be hard to read, but a good baseline rule is a 1:17 ratio of coffee:water. Depending on your exact method for extraction, this ratio may vary.

The water needs to be 195-205 degrees F (90-96 C) for optimal extraction. Lower tempratures will result in flat, underdeveloped flavors, while higher temperatures will extract undesired compounds.

Time in contact with the water will vary based on the particle size of the coffee you are using. It can vary from anywhere between about 8 seconds for espresso, to steeping for about 3 minutes for French press. The time is only a general trend, and will of course also vary based on the exact method you are using.


How to grind coffee

Burr grinders are preffered for grinding coffee (shop for burr grinder here). A burr grinder works by the action of a toothed plate that spins at a set distance from a second toothed plate, which is fixed in place. The coffee beans are poured into the center, and make their way towards the edges, and can only fit through between the edges of the plate once they have been ground to a sufficently small size. Once they have reached the edge, the grounds will fall into a collection hopper, and be away from any further grinding. Burr grinders are used in all professional applications where coffee is ground. If you're serious about your coffee, the investment in a decent home grade burr grinder is well worth the expense. Blade grinders mostly work, and are much less expensive. They generate a very irregular particle size, which is less than ideal for any sort of coffee application.

Methods for making coffee


French Press see more

A standard size French press carafe will hold 32 ounces total. After the grounds are in, that leaves room for about 30 ounces of water. I weigh out 1.8 ounces of beans for my French press.

Start by grinding fresh coffee coarsely.

Pour them into your press.

Boil some water in your kettle, give it a minute to cool from 212 to about 205, pour the water over your grounds, give it a stir with a wooden or plastic utensil.

Set a timer for 3 minutes.

Put the lid on, and adjust the plunger so that the grounds are held under the surface of the water. This helps to ensure that all the grounds are held under the surface of the water.

When your time is up, gently but firmly push the plunger down and pour.


Espresso
We don't recommend trying to make espresso at home. Proper espresso requires an extremely temperature stable source of water, which means a big boiler. It also requires enormously high pressure to pull properly, and to extract all of the desired compounds from the grounds, 9 bars of pressure to be exact. Espresso is also the trickiest method of coffee making, needing constant adjustment of both the grinder and of the machine itself. It is so tricky, in fact, that many baristas aren't able to do a decent job with it. There are plenty of "home espresso&auot; machines out there, but few of them make a drink that bears more than a passing resemblance to espresso. If you can't live without your espresso fix at home, you have two good options. The first is the Spaziale Vivaldi S1, a high quality pro-sumer machine that makes pretty good espresso. The second doesn't make espresso, but makes a concentrated coffee extract which is as closer than most home espresso machines can make.

Pourover see more

Start by grinding fresh coffee finely.

Pour your grounds into a handy container.

Boil some water in your kettle, give it a minute to cool from 212 to about 205.

Place your paper filter into your brewer. Pour a little water onto it to rinse your filter and pre-warm your mug.

Pour out the water in your mug.

Pour the grounds into your filter, then slowly pour the water from your kettle into the grounds, making sure to wet them completely.

Don't let all of the water drain out from the brewer until you are finished. This can clog the filter with fines. Just keep topping it with water from your kettle every few seconds.

Set the brewer in the sink to finish dripping.

Enjoy your coffee.


Automatic drip

Most automatic drip machines have some issues. They can't make particularly good coffee for two reasons. First, they only get the water to about 185 degrees. Second, they cannot hold enough coffee to make the proper strength brew. Yes, this is actually true. For the full 60 ounces in a 12 cup maker, you should have about 3.25 ounces of coffee grounds, which would overfill the basket.

Here are some directions for getting the most out of your automatic drip maker.

First, grind some coffee to a medium grind. Use a finer grind if you have a cone filter, or a coarser grind if you are using a mesh basket.

Put a filter into the brewing basket, and fill about halfway with fresh grounds. Don't fill it further than halfway, since the coffee needs room to mix with water. If you overfill the basket, it will overflow and make a giant mess.

Fill the carafe about halfway -- to the 6 cup mark in most makers.

Pour into the reservoir and turn it on.

Wait until it's done making noise, then turn off the hot plate and enjoy your coffee! You can pour it into a thermos if you want to keep your coffee hot for longer than 20 minutes or so. Pro tip: Do not use the "steal a cup" feature. It will not only wind you up with a poor cup of coffee, it will also degrade the entire pot.


AeroPress see more

Grind some coffee (17 grams) to a fine grind.

Note: this recipe does not follow the general 1:17 rule; it makes a concentrated extract similar to espresso.

Place a paper filter into the filter basket.

Turn your aero press upside down, slide the plunger to the bottom of the 4 circle.

Pour your grounds into the body of your aero press.

Boil some water, then give it a minute to cool to about 205.

Pour a little water into your basket to rinse off the filter and pre-warm your mug, then empty your mug.

Pour water in up to the top of the 2 circle in your press.

Give it a quick stir, clap the basket on, and invert over the mug.

Press the plunger down. Don't push too hard or fast, it can clog the filter with fines.

Pull the basket off and push the plunger all the way though to pop the puck out.

Top your mug with hot water (if desired).

Coffee!


Camping Colador see more

This is my favorite way to make coffee when I don't have my French press. I use it when camping or sometimes when I need more than the 30 ounces I can make in the press. The beauty of this method is that it can be scaled up as much as you like; you just need a bag big enough to catch the grounds and a container big enough to catch the coffee.

Start with a simple bag sewn out of muslin.

Grind some coffee coarsely (remember our 1:17 ratio).

Note: I like to make about 26 ounces of coffee at a time, so I start with 1.5 ounces grounds.

Boil some water, and let it cool for a minute from 212 to 205.

Place the colador into a second pot (or bottle or mug).

Pour your measured grounds into the colador.

Pour water over your grounds. Let it steep for three minutes. As you pour the water, the edges of your cloth bag might try to get away into your coffee. It needs to be held in place. Be very careful to either wear a glove in this stage or to not pour hot water over your hand. I sometimes use a rubber band to hold the bag in place.

Pull the colador out of the coffee, let drip for a few seconds.

Shake out the grounds, and rinse the colador.

Coffee!


Colador see more

where to buy a Colador This works just like a camping colador, simply follow the same directions, using your purchased colador instead of your musilin bag.

A downside of this method is that it can't scale up, but an upside is that if you buy a colador, it will have a handle built in. This can save you some scaleded fingers if you aren't careful pouring hot water.


Ibrik, Cezve, Briki see more

An Ibrik is used to make Turkish or Greek coffee. This is a style of coffee common to the Middle East, and is known by many names. I really like it as an afternoon or evening coffee to accompany conversation, especially if served along with a hookah.

Grind coffee as finely as possible. A blade grinder will not work, as it leaves some particles much too large. Most home burr grinders won't work either as they leave ALL the particles slightly too large. You should use a turkish coffee grinder or another hand mill, like the Hario mini mill pictured to the right. It's especially important that the coffee be ground very finely, as in this preparation the grounds will float to the surface if they are too large.

It will go stale very quickly, so wait to grind until you are about to brew your coffee.

Pour cold water into the ibrik, 2 ounces per serving. For each serving, add one tsp. sugar, then one heaping tsp. grounds on top. Do not stir yet.

Heat the pot slowly, over medium heat.

Watch closely, and when it starts to foam up, take it off the heat and stir down.

Put the ibrik back on the heat, let it foam back up, and stir down again.

Repeat a third time, but do not stir down.

Pour into cups.

Flavor with a knife tip of turkish coffee spice (optional).

Coffee!


Cold brew

Mix one cup of coarse ground coffee with four cups water in a quart jar. Let stand for 12-24 hours. Strain thorugh a colador or cheesecloth or muslin bag.

It will keep in the refrigerator for up to two weeks.

Note: this recipe does not follow the general 1:17 rule, but makes a concentrated extract.

Mix 1:2 or 1:3 with water. before serving.

This method for making coffee is unsuitable for drinking hot, it will result in a flat, boring cup of coffee with virtually no acidity. (Many people think it makes tasty iced coffee.)

Some companies that build equipment specifically designed to make cold press coffee make false claims that their coffee has "67% less acid" than other coffees. Do not fall for this hype. Coffee has less acid than many common beverages, including tomato juice, lemonade, and Coca-Cola. See my article here.
