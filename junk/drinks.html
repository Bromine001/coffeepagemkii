Common coffee drinks.

This is not meant to be an exhaustive list, merely representative of common drinks you are likely to encounter.

Americano

Espresso mixed with hot water, to approximate the strength of brewed coffee. Common dilutions range anywhere from 1:2 to 1:10 of espresso to water. Originally invented in Italy, where American soldiers on leave wanted cups of brewed coffee, but the Italian cafes only had espresso machines.

Black Eye

A synonym for a Red Eye. See Red Eye.

Breve

Technically any coffee drink made with half and half, more typical usage is a latte made with half and half instead of milk.

Cafe au lait

Brewed coffee topped with steamed milk. Generally served in a 1:1 ratio.

Cowboy coffee

A method of making coffee, where water is boiled, then grounds added, then allowed to settle but not removed before drinking. There are various methods for minimizing the grounds in the cup including cracking a raw egg into the pot, adding an ice cube, and tapping the side of the pot.

None of these methods is particularly effective. Due to the continued presence of grounds, we don't recommend this method of preparation.

Cubano

Specifically it is espresso sweetened with sugar added into the portafilter.

Pro tip: This is a foolish practice which not only makes a terrible shot of espresso, but will wear out an espresso machine prematurely. A very similar effect can be more effectively achieved by pouring sugar into the shot glass to dissolve as the espresso drips out.

Cappuccino

A mix of espresso and steamed milk. Generally uses much stiffer foam than a latte. Traditionally cappuccinos are served in a 1:1:1 ratio between espresso: liquid milk: milk foam. This generates a 5-6 ounce drink. In the US, a cappuccino is often served in a 16 or 20 ounce size, generally with one or two shots of espresso, much more like a stiff latte than a traditional cappuccino.

Cortado

Espresso mixed with warm milk in a 1:1 ratio. Often drunk by people who like espresso but don't care for the strong flavor of straight espresso.

Doppio

Italian for "double". Used in the US as a pretentious term for a double shot of espresso.

Espresso

The base of nearly every coffee drink. See the section on espresso for more information.

Flat white

A latte with no foam. Popular in Australia, becoming popular in the US also.

French Press

An extraction method rather than a drink per se. In a French press (or Bodum, or plunger press), the grounds are separated using a fine metal screen rather than a paper filter. The resultant coffee is thicker, with a more robust body than the same coffee made in a drip maker. Click here for a photo essay about making french press coffee.

Greek Coffee

See Turkish coffee

Green Eye

A synonym for a Red Eye. See Red Eye.

Instant coffee

Not a beverage. It is in fact, not fit for human consumption.

Latte (Cafe latte)

The most common coffee drink in the US. A latte is a mix of espresso and steamed milk. Traditionally in a 1:3 ratio, though more common in the US is anywhere between a 1:5 and 1:16 ratio. Often served with flavored sugar syrup or chocolate.

Lattes are generally made with either one or two shots of espresso. Generally served in 8, 12, 16, or 20 oz. sizes.

Mocha (Cafe mocha)

A variant of a latte with chocolate added. These are served at a variety of sweetnesses and strengths, sometimes with dark chocolate, and sometimes with white chocolate.

"Turtle" lattes are a variant made with caramel and chocolate.

"Nutella" lattes are another variant made with hazlenut syrup and chocolate.

Macchiato

Espresso with a scoop of milk foam on top. Usually either a single or double shot, served in a demitasse cup.

Note: A "Caramel Macchiato" is a Starbucks drink, not a coffee shop drink. To get a Starbucks Caramel Macchiato in a different coffee shop, you should order a caramel vanilla latte.

"Low Acid" coffee

A bulls**t marketing gimmick that preys on people who are misinformed about the difference between the flavor "acidity" and the pH of coffee. Coffee is far less acidic than Lemonade, Coca-Cola, or tomatoes. See my article about acidity in coffee.

Moka

Coffee made with a Moka pot. Similar to espresso, but made with much lower pressure than espresso.

Pour over

Like the French Press, a pour over is really an extraction method rather than a drink. A pour over is basically brewed coffee. It only differs in that it is made by hand, generally with a Melitta or a Hario V-60.

Click here for a photo essay about making pour over coffee.

Percolator

An old fashioned method for brewing coffee, rather than a drink. A percolator is a pot with a plate in the bottom hooked to a central tube that extends to the top of the pot. As water boils, it is forced up the tube to splash repeatedly over a basket of grounds which is also suspended in the pot over the level of the water.

As this method involves re-circulating the coffee through the basket, and using water which is at 212 deg. We do not reccomend it as a preparation method. It tends to lead to a badly overextracted coffee, which is extremely bitter.

Red Eye(Green Eye, Black Eye, etc.)

Brewed coffee with espresso. Generally one shot for a Red Eye.

Pro tip: Your Barista hates it when you order some "Black Eye" "Green Eye" etc. Your Barista loves it when you order "A 16 oz. coffee with two shots of espresso and room for cream."

Ristretto

Espresso pulled "short", generally about .75 oz per shot rather than the usual 1 oz. Generally generates a richer drink with more acidity and more floral or citrus notes and less bittersweet caramel flavor.

Shot in the dark

A synonym for a Red Eye. See Red Eye.

Turkish coffee

A very strong coffee made with an ibrik, traditionally drunk black with sugar. Often cardamom and/or other spices are added. This preperation leaves the grounds in the bottom of the serving cup.

Click here for a turkish coffee photo essay.

Vietnamese iced coffee

Strong coffee (often brewed with chicory) or espresso with sweetened condensed milk poured over ice. Also called Thai iced coffee.
